Historical Context of the Virginia Woolf Portrait

Historical Context of the Virginia Woolf Portrait

This portrait of Virginia Woolf was taken in 1902 by George Charles Beresford, a prominent photographer known for his portraits of literary figures. The photograph captures Woolf at age 20, well before she would publish her first novel, The Voyage Out (1915).

At the time this photograph was taken, Woolf was living at 22 Hyde Park Gate with her father, Leslie Stephen, following the death of her mother in 1895. This period preceded the formation of the influential Bloomsbury Group, which would later establish Woolf as a central figure in modernist literature.

The portrait shows the young writer before her struggles with mental illness intensified, though she had already experienced periods of nervous breakdown following family bereavements. The contemplative expression and direct gaze present a woman of serious intellectual purpose, characteristics that would define her literary career.

This image is particularly significant as it documents Virginia Woolf before she adopted her married name and before her literary fame. It captures a transitional moment in her life, between her sheltered Victorian upbringing and her emergence as a pioneering modernist author.

Places Related to Virginia Woolf

Places

  • London — Capital of the United Kingdom; frequent setting in Virginia Woolf's life and work.
  • St Ives — Seaside town in Cornwall; Virginia Woolf used to live there and it inspired Woolf's To the Lighthouse coastal setting.
  • Rodmell — A village in East Sussex where Virginia Woolf lived at Monk’s House with her husband Leonard Woolf; it was her country retreat and the place where she wrote or revised several major works, including Mrs Dalloway, To the Lighthouse, Orlando and so on. Tragically, it was also the place where she took her own life in 1941.

The Hours: Virginia Woolf’s Modern Echo

The Hours: Virginia Woolf’s Modern Echo

The film The Hours (2002) interweaves three parallel narratives to explore the enduring legacy of Virginia Woolf’s writing. One storyline portrays Woolf herself—struggling to compose Mrs. Dalloway—played by Nicole Kidman. The second follows Laura Brown (played by Julianne Moore), a 1950s housewife who reads Woolf’s novel and contemplates her own life choices. The third narrative centers on Clarissa Vaughan (played by Meryl Streep), a contemporary New Yorker planning a party for her friend Richard, a poet dying of suicide. The film's title refers to the concept of time and the hours that shape our lives, echoing Woolf's exploration of consciousness and the passage of time in her own work.
